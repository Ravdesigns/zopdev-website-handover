Chapter 01 · p. 04–08

Methodology

How we anonymised, sampled, and validated 4,200 production cloud accounts across AWS, GCP, and Azure — without ever reading customer secrets, application data, or billing line items tied to identity.

The dataset behind this report is built from anonymised customer accounts on the ZopDev platform. Each account was sampled at hourly resolution across 31 days, then aggregated to monthly medians and 90th-percentile bounds. We did not query secrets, customer application data, or any field that could re-identify an organisation. Every aggregation step was reviewed by an independent privacy engineer before being committed to the analysis branch.

We over-sampled non-production environments by design. The thesis going in was that non-prod is where 24/7 idle accumulates fastest — that turned out to be correct, but the magnitude surprised even us. We’ll get to the headline number in Chapter 02.

Why this dataset is unusual

Most cloud-cost studies sample synthetic workloads or a small number of opt-in customers. Ours samples real production deployments at scale — the per-cloud distribution is large enough to talk about medians and bounds with confidence.

Sampling frame

Accounts qualify if they have at least 30 days of continuous billing data, at least one production environment tagged, and at least one non-production environment that ran during a typical work week. We excluded accounts where engineers had already enabled aggressive scheduling on day zero — those skew the “before” baseline.

Validation

Every aggregate number in this report was cross-checked against a reconstructed bill from the cloud provider’s native cost API. Where the reconstruction disagreed with our internal model by more than 2 %, the account was excluded. 211 accounts were dropped this way, leaving the 4,200 in the final cohort.

Chapter 02 · p. 09–17

The shape of waste

Five categories of non-production waste, ranked by absolute dollar size across the cohort. The order isn’t the one most teams predict.

When we ask engineering leaders to rank these categories before reading the report, the most common ordering puts “idle instances” first by a wide margin. That’s wrong — schedule gaps are #1 by total dollars, idle instances are #2. Over-provisioned production lands at #3, ahead of orphan resources and unclaimed rate optimisation.

The five categories are stable across clouds — AWS and GCP and Azure all show the same ranking. The magnitudes differ (Azure has more idle-storage waste; GCP has more orphan IPs) but the order of operations is the same. We dig into per-cloud differences in Chapter 07.

Chapter 03 · p. 18–26

Idle resources

The forgotten EC2, RDS, EKS, and Cloud SQL workloads that nobody owns — and what we observed when teams ran their first audit.

An idle resource is one whose median CPU + network utilisation over the last 14 days is under 3 % during business hours and effectively zero outside them. By that definition, 22 % of non-prod compute hours in our cohort were attributable to idle resources. Most owners didn’t know they existed.

The pattern repeats: a team spins up a sandbox for a one-off integration test, the project gets de-prioritised, the sandbox stays running. Multiply by a typical 24-month tenure for a senior engineer and you have a long tail of forgotten boxes. The fix is rarely deletion — it’s scheduled stop-start, paired with an ownership audit.

“The hardest part wasn’t finding the idle resources. It was getting the original creator to admit they were idle.” — Anonymous platform lead, Fortune 500 SaaS
Chapter 04 · p. 27–35

Right-sizing

Family-by-family analysis with confidence bounds. T3, M5, R5, and the burstable trap that costs more than it saves.

Right-sizing recommendations are the most-ignored category in the dataset. Teams know it works in principle, but the per-instance change feels small and the workflow to apply it touches infrastructure-as-code repos owned by busy people. The aggregate is what matters: across the cohort, the median over-provisioned EC2 instance was 3.4× the right size for its 14-day workload.

The burstable trap is real. Teams pick t3.large because the documentation says it’s cheaper, then run sustained workloads on it for months while accumulating CPU credit debt. By the time the alarm fires, the savings have evaporated. The 90-day analysis in Chapter 04’s appendix shows the break-even line by workload type.

Chapter 05 · p. 36–42

Orphan cleanup

Volumes, snapshots, NAT gateways, idle IPs. Day-one savings, no risk — if you can answer “who created this?”

Orphan resources are the easiest money in the report. They’re not attached to anything; they have no live workload depending on them; their owners have usually moved teams or companies. The median monthly cost of orphan resources per account in our cohort was $180 — small per resource, fat tail in aggregate.

The friction isn’t technical, it’s political. Deleting an orphan EBS volume requires someone to vouch that the data on it isn’t needed for a future restore. Most teams don’t have a clean process for that, so the orphans persist. The 30-day playbook in Chapter 08 walks through one approach that worked across nine of the studied accounts.

Chapter 06 · p. 43–49

Scheduling impact

The 70 % reduction we measured in non-production spend, broken down by environment type and team size.

Scheduling is the highest-ROI intervention in the report — not because it’s clever, but because non-production environments don’t need to run 168 hours a week when a 9-to-6 team uses them 50. The math is brutal: even modest scheduling (off nights + weekends) recovers ~60 % of non-prod spend on the eligible resource types.

Smaller teams save more, surprisingly. Teams under 25 engineers had a median scheduling-driven reduction of 73 %; teams over 200 came in at 56 %. The difference isn’t about the platform — it’s about how many ambiguous “always-on” carve-outs each team has accumulated.

Chapter 07 · p. 50–57

Per-cloud breakdown

AWS vs GCP vs Azure waste profiles. The categories are stable; the magnitudes are very different.

The shape of waste is consistent across clouds — same five categories, same ordering by dollar size. The magnitudes diverge: AWS accounts in our cohort showed the highest idle-compute waste; GCP accounts had the most orphan static IPs and load balancers; Azure accounts had the largest idle-storage tail (a function of the snapshot lifecycle defaults).

Multi-cloud accounts (about 14 % of the cohort) sat in the middle on every category — not because they were more disciplined, but because waste distributed across three providers is harder to see in any one bill.

Chapter 08 · p. 58–64

The 30-day playbook

Where to start, by size of cloud bill. A sequenced 30-day plan for under-$50K, $50K–$500K, and over-$500K monthly spend.

The data is interesting; the action items are what move the bill. The playbook is sequenced so the early days produce verifiable wins (orphan cleanup, scheduling on obvious targets), and the later days do the slower right-sizing work that benefits from a stable 14-day baseline.

Under-$50K accounts can usually compress this to a single sprint. $50K–$500K accounts should expect three weeks of platform-team time. Over-$500K accounts need a steering committee and an explicit rollback policy — a 1 % production miss at that scale is more expensive than the right-sizing savings.
